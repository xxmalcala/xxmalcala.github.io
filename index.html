Eukaryotic Genome Evolution and Biodiversity

Bridging 2+ billion years to explore the evolution of eukaryotes, their genomes,
and diversification through the lense of microbial eukaryotes

Placing Eukaryotes in Context

Constructing the eukaryotic tree of life (eToL) presents a unique on-going challenge as new deeply diverging lineages are described each year. While keeping pace with emergent ancient lineages is challenging, construction of a robust eToL is critically important for studying the evolution of eukaryotes and their subsequent diversification. My work aims to reconcile multiple approaches to infer robust relationships at challenging nodes in the eToL (e.g., the monophyly of "Archaeplastida" and "Excavata") as well as "orphan" lineages that likely have importance regarding the "root" of the eukaryotic tree of life (particularly Malawimonads). This work includes incorporating multiple phylogenomic techniques and forms of evidence, aiming to generate robust hypotheses to explore eukaryotic evolution from the last common ancestor (~1.8 billion years ago) to now!

How does genetic novelty arise and at what pace?

Phylogenomics provides a criticial means to explore the evolution and subsequent diversification of genetic novelty at many scales, from sibling species, phyla, 'supergroups' and ultimately to major domains of life. Building upon PhyloToL, my work has aimed to develop approaches and tools to aid in understanding the evolution of young genes and gene families from predominantly single-cell 'omics data through phylogenomic and machine learning approaches. This avenue of work is making it feasible to explore the evolution of genetic novelty, from de novo genes to inferring the evolutionary pacing of gene family birth/death and expansion/contract over deep time scales. With an emphasis on lineages of eukaryotes where single-cell 'omics approaches dominate the available pools of data (e.g., ciliates and amoebozoans), there is ample opportunity to understand the evolutionary biology of genetic 'novelty' across eukaryotes and its contributions to the on-going diversification of major eukaryotic lineages.

Linking Genome Biology to Genome Evolution

When considering genome evolution, we often overlook the impacts of organismal biology and life history. Using ciliates, my work has focused on how their unusual genome biology (including developmental rearrangements and somatic hyperpolyploidy) manifest in their genome evolution. While ciliates represent extremes, their genome and developmental biology manifest in their evolution in interesting ways; extreme genome processing is tied to relaxed evolutionary pressures (Yan, Maurer-Alcalá, et al., 2019) and may impact gene family birth/death too (Maurer-Alcalá, et al., 2018, Maurer-Alcalá, et al., In Press). This work has bridged diverse areas of biology (development, evolution, bioinformatics) and I am aiming to further explore these questions using experimental evolution and molecular biology techniques.

Developing Bioinformatic Approaches and Tools

Phylogenomics and comparative 'omics often use increasingly large datasets that present unique computational challenges. An important aspect of my work involves the design and development of computational tools and approaches that improve access to those with less computational expertise. This includes PhyloToL, a phylogenomic pipeline with wide applications , tools for comparing genomes in ciliates (crazy microeukaryotes) to machine learning tools to aid in data processing and phylogenomics!
